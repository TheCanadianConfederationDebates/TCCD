He was in favor of Rail- ways and did not wish to be misunderstood onthat question, but when they were undertakenprovisions should be made fro them. The policyhad not been brought forward to take us on tothe Dominion, but he would warn the Govern-ment that such would be the result. If we gotinto financial difficulty there would be no help forus but to ask Canada to help us out. He hadnever advocated Confederation ; there had beenno terms offered that he would accept. It Anti-Confederates got into trouble and financial diffi-culty they could not blame him. The Govern-ment might perhaps come down with a supple-mentary Revenue Bill and thus make provisionfor the construction of the Branches. He wouldpledge himself in any shape or form to vote forthe Act. He would like to see the people of theWest and East enjoying the blessing and priv-ileges of the Railway. He believed that thepeople of Tignish required a railway. It was apopulous district and he supposed a Branch therewould pay very well. The Eastern Branch wouldnot pay directly but would do so indirectly byopening up the Country. He considered that theBranches should be built but opposed the mannerto which the Bill for the construction had beendrawn up, making as it did no provision for itspayment.

Hon. Leader of the Government did not intend to make a long speech at the present stated. Theleader of the opposition had made a differentspeech from that delivered by him last year. He(Mr. P.) was still of the opinion that the 2 1-2per cent would carry out the provisions of theoriginal Bill. As far as the present Bill wasconcerned no alarm need be experienced as to theparting of the Country to any expense that was notprovided for. As debentures would not requireto be issued till the next Revenue Bill would bepassed. The hon. member had said that themain line would be self-sustaining - he would liketo see his prediction verified. He consideredthat it was a very heavy burden to be lain onthe people without consulting them. For so do-ing they had gone to the wall, but a majority hadbeen returned to carry out their policy detri-mental as it might be to the best interests of theIsland. The burden was not like an in-cubus of debt imposed by war, as it wasundertaken for the improvement of thecountry. He would allow that a railway

benefitted any country in which it was constructed, to a certain extent. Hewould say that if the 2 1/2 per cent wouldpay for the construction of the Main Line,any Government would be justified inbuilding the Branches, for if 2 1/2 per cent,would pay for the construction of 140miles, 1 1/2 would certainly pay for thebuilding of 50. This would only raiseour duty to a level with that of the Do-minion. He had understood from theEngineers that the Line would be in oper-ation from Charlottetown to Summersidein six months. That portion would showhow much it would pay, and how much trafficwould be on the line. The Branches hadcome to be a matter of necessity. NoGovernment would be able to exist here-after that would not give a promise of aBranch. He believed it would be aheavier undertaking for this Island thanthe Canadian railways were to that coun-try. But even then, it would be betterto carry our own burdens, than to give upour self-Government and go to Canda forassistance. He considered however, thatthat country would be in quite as difficulta position as we would be in, before allher Railways were finished. The hon.member for Alberton had spoken of theMinute of Council and said that was thefirst time a railway had been spoken of. -We were necessitated to speak about it. -He believed that Minute of Council hadsettled the question of Confederation, asno further proposals had been received. -We had shown them that the British Am-erican Act was unjust to this Island, in-asmuchas as we were an Island and wouldhave to pay for our own public works,that Act declaring that only those workswhich connected two provinces should bebuilt from the general Revenue. TheCanadians had replied to our representa-tions, through their Colonial Minister,that they considered we stood in our ownlight. His opinion was that if the inhab-itants were true to their interests theynever would be forced into Confederation.
