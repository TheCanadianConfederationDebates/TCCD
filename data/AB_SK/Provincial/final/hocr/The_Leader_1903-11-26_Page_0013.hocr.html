REASONS FOR REFUSING THE ADVANCE.

The larger question had been raisedas to whether or not they should spendthe $250,000 held up before them bythe Dominion Parliament. For onehe said they should not. It would beunsound business to touch it. Therewere several reasons against acceptingit. First, until our just demands hadbeen satisfactorily met by the federalGovernment. Second, because itmeant a direct lessening of the federalexpenditure in this country. TheDominion Government was paying forthe two bridges in the west which hadbeen referred to but what happenedthe other day when a deputation wentto Ottawa and asked for the construc-tion of a large traffic bridge at Saska-toon ? Sir Wilfrid Laurier simplysaid " Go to Regina and they willbuild it for you out of the capitaladvance." That meant that instead ofbuilding the bridge and charging thecost against Canada as a whole itwould be charged against the capitalaccount of the Territories. Here inthe West we had no canals or harborsto be constructed as public works bythe Dominion and in this country hecontended that these large bridgesshould be considered public works forthe benefit of Canada and the whole ofCanada should pay their share towardsthem in the same way as the people inthe West were paying their share fordigging canals, erecting lighthouses,dredging harbors, etc., in the oldprovinces. A third reason was that itwas fastening a loan on the people ofthe Territories on terms about whichthey had nothing to say. With pro-vincial autonomy they could borrowmoney at least as low as four per cent.While they would get the sum offeredwithout interest now it would ulti-mately cost them $12,500 per annumfor ever. In the early days in theWestern States the States and Terri-tories enjoyed the power of borrowingmoney and pledging the public creditand so confident were they of thefuture that they paid as high as tenper cent per annum and the invest-ment had proved a good one. Fourth,it would mean putting off the grantingof provincial institutions. The Domin-ion would take the position of the federalMembers that it was merely a questionof money and not of principle. Fifth,the very fact of being styled Territoriesinstead of having the dignity of a

province was an injury and a mangoing abroad to do business would soonrealise the great advantage of comingfrom a province rather than a terri-tory. The business men today werewriting at the head of their letterpaper "Regina, Canada," instead of" Regina, N.W.T.," or " Assiniboia."It was difficult to divest oneself entirely of party bias in dealing with this ques-tion but he had endeavoured to do so.In conclusion he appealed to his Liberalfriends not to allow party exigenciesto outweigh the country's interests.The constituencies which had returnedthem before to press for autonomywould return them again. When he first came into the Legis-lature, nine years ago, he supportedthe North-West Government in theirdemands at Ottawa, both for increasedfinancial assistance and extendedlegislative powers. He believed thatour present Premier, Mr. Haultain, atthat time was just as sincere and justas reasonable in making his demandsthen as he was during his last mis-sion to Ottawa and he believed thatit was the intention of the then Gov-ernment at Ottawa to treat the Terri-tories fairly, and he also believed thatthe present Government desire to treatthe Territories fairly. Nine years agoonly 59 per cent. of our financial de-mands were granted, while over 20 percent. more than that has beengranted this year not taking intoaccount this capital advance. " Ido not desire," said Mr. Brown, "toshow that the then Government atOttawa was any more unfair to us thanthe present Government, but the pointis that the action of this House has hadan educating influence on Parliament.This is not only true regarding our fin-ancial demands, but it is true in othermatters as well. C.P.R. LAND TAX EXEMPTION.

He wished to differ from the state-ment made by the member for WestCalgary in connection with the ques-tion of the exemption of the CanadianPacific Railway lands from school tax-ation. The member for West Calgaryhad stated that this was a matter of noimportance : that when schools wereneeded in any particular district it wasnot until practically all the railwaylands had been sold and that the set-tlers had to pay the taxes. While hedid not believe that this statementeven was correct, because there werevery many school districts situated inparts of the country where practicallyall the land paying taxes were evensections, still, supposing that the state-ment of the member for West Calgarywas correct, he differed very widelyfrom him. Some years ago the prin-

ciple of taxing all the land in thiscountry for the purposes of local im-provements was established. It wasargued that if the settlers by cominginto this country, building roads andbridges, establishing schools and goodgovernment were giving to the vacantlands a value, they were doing a workwhich ought to be supplemented by ataxation of all lands throughout theTerritories, however remote, whichwere being enhanced in value by theexpenditure of the moneys belongingto the people then in the country.The principle that all lands which re-ceive benefit from taxation should paytaxes was thus established, and whatwas true, he argued, in connectionwith local improvement taxation, wasto a greater extent true in connectionwith schools. If this country wasproud to be able to invite men to maketheir homes in it on the ground thatin every fairly settled district theywould have schools at their doors,which in efficiency would be equal tothose where they were leaving,he thought it was a still greatermatter of congratulation to thepeople of this country to saythat if settlers should come to itwho should find it necessary to locatethemselves in comparatively sparselysettled districts that they there wouldalso have the same advantagesbelonging to the old establishedsettlements in this country. In order to sustain schools under thesecircumstances without imposing aburden too heavy to be borne by theenterprising pioneer, it is necessarythat every acre of land so benefited,whether directly or indirectly, shouldpay a tax, which while not heavyenough to be a burden to anyonewould be of substantial assistance inassisting those settlers who arestruggling to establish the institutionsso much prized in the land they camefrom. He thought it was farmore important that the districtsparsely settled where very littlerailway land had been sold should beassisted in their initial stages thanthat even assistance should be given tothe old and established settlements,who, having overcome the earlydifficulties and having becomecomparatively well off, would feel nodifficulty in maintaining efficientschools.

He did not take any stock at all inthe argument that we shouldcongratulate ourselves that the peoplehere have the privilege of paying allthe taxes that are necessary to makethis country an attractive one to theimmigrant and that the people who areparticularly interested in the coming here of the immigrants in order thatthey may sell their lands at goodprices should be allowed to go free.This line was hardly in keeping withone who had so loudly proclaimedhimself a champion of the rights of theNorth-West settlers.

THE SASKATOON BRIDGE.

Mr. Brown then took up Mr. Ben-nett's argument which that gentle-man based on the Saskatoon bridge.The hon. gentleman from West Cal-gary has instanced the construction bythe Dominion Government of thenumberless small harbors and wharvesin the Maritime Provinces built by theDominion Government solely for thepurpose of pandering to a certain vote.He did not consider the case at allanalogous. There is no doubtthat if the capital advance hadbeen accepted the Saskatoon bridgewould have been built and charg-ed to the capital account. If thecapital advance had not been voted theSaskatoon bridge might have beenbuilt by the Dominion Government andit might not have been. For fifteenyears this bridge has been a greatnecessity but up to the present noGovernment at Ottawa has seen fit tomake the expenditure from the consol-idated revenues of the Dominion. Inthe case of the province of Manitoba,many such bridges have been built andmost, if not all of them, have been paidby the province and the cost of themhave become part of the debt of the Province of Manitoba, and the proba-bility is that the Dominion Govern-ment would expect this and similarbridges to be built in the same way.The hon. member from West Calgaryinstanced the bridge across the St.Lawrence. Surely he did not mean tosay that a bridge across the Saskat-chewan river at Saskatoon would be awork of the same nature as a bridgeacross the St. Lawrence river nearQuebec. Again, I say, that while I donot deny that if the Dominion Govern-ment had not placed at our disposalthe sum of $250,000, to be used on capi-tal account that they might have builtthis bridge, I do say that there wasno great probability of this work beingtaken up as a Dominion work and thatif we had accepted the capital expendi-ture the bridge would have been a cer-tainty, as well as other works whichare urgently needed by the Territoriesand through whose noncompletion weare suffering serious inconvenience andhindrance to the business of severaldistricts in the Territories.
