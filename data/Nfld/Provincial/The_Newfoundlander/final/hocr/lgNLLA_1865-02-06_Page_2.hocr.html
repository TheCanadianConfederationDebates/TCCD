THE Address in reply to the Governor's Speech has not yet passed through the Assembly, where it has been the subject of considerable discussion, especially as regards the absorbing question of Confederation. On Thursday the Delegates, Messrs. CARTER and SHEA, addressed the House at very considerable length in explanation of the question, and of the rea­sons which induced them to regard it with approval. We think no dispassionate man could have heard them without feeling they had made a strong case, and placed the subject in a clear and comprehensive light before the House. This, we believe, ras the impression they produced, and we do know that some who had been hostile to the measure were satisfied by the reasons put forward that not only might the coun­try safely adopt it, but that it would be unwise and suicidal to reject it. We regret that owing to the great imperfection of cur system of reporting, some days must elapse before these speeches are before the public in anything like completeness. The Militia scarecrow and the “bleaching of bones on the Canadian soil,” have been pretty well laughed out of value for any further purposes of sensation.
